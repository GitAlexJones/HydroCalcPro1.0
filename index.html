[CAM] Camera Flow Detection
// Point camera at sump crest — auto-measure water head and calculate flow rate

This feature uses your camera + computer vision to auto-detect crest height (H) and calculate flow.

The 7-Step Camera Pipeline:

  1. Start Camera — accesses your device camera (rear-facing preferred) and draws live video to an HTML canvas — a digital drawing board that updates ~30 times per second so we can analyze each frame as raw pixel data
  2. Calibrate — two orange lines appear on the video feed. You use the slider controls below the video to position them over a known distance (recommended: 6 inches for best accuracy — only ~1% error vs ~6% with 1 inch). Enter the distance and click "Set Scale." This establishes the pixels-per-inch conversion. Tape a 6" ruler section on the sump pipe wall for a permanent reference
  3. Auto-Detection (Sobel Edge Detector) — every frame, the app converts the image to grayscale, then runs a Sobel operator that detects horizontal edges by comparing brightness above and below each pixel. It accumulates edge strength across the center 60% of each row, then finds the two strongest horizontal edges: the crest line (red — top of the pipe rim) and the water surface (cyan — where reflections meet the wall)
  4. H Measurement — the pixel distance between the crest and water surface is converted to inches using your calibration: H_inches = (y_water − y_crest) ÷ pixels_per_inch. This is displayed as a live orange arrow on the video with the measurement label
  5. 30-Frame Averaging — the H buffer holds the last 30 readings and averages them to eliminate wave noise. At 30 fps, this smooths over exactly 1 second of video: H_avg = sum(last 30 readings) ÷ 30
  6. Dual Flow Calculation — the averaged H feeds into BOTH the weir equation (Q = Cw × L × H^1.5 → GPM) AND the hatchery chart interpolation. Both values display on the dark HUD below the video so you can cross-check
  7. Snap Reading — captures the current averaged measurement into the history table with reading number, H value, both GPM calculations, frame count, and timestamp. The buffer resets for a fresh next reading

The Live HUD shows three values:

[THEORY] Technical Reference
// The science behind every hatchery flow calculation

Where Does This Math Come From?

Every calculation traces back to the Continuity Equation from the Conservation of Mass — established by Leonhard Euler and Daniel Bernoulli in the 1700s.

[i]
In plain English: what goes in must come out. If you know the pipe size and water speed, you can calculate exactly how much water flows. If you know the volume and flow, you can calculate how long it takes to replace the water.

Measuring Guidelines (from Class)

[+]
Always record units — say "25 cm" not "25"
Measure diameter first → divide by 2 for radius
Convert units BEFORE formulas
Round at the FINAL step only
Measure twice, check with a partner
[!]
Common Errors: Start at 0 not the tool edge • Keep tape straight • Read meniscus at eye level • Measure through center for diameters • Interior vs exterior dimensions

The Key Formulas

Surface Area

Rectangle: A = L × WCircle: A = π × r² (r = diameter ÷ 2)

Volume

Rectangle/Raceway: V = L × W × HCylinder: V = π × r² × hCone: V = ⅓ × π × r² × hCone-Bottom Pond: V = (πr²×h₂) + (⅓πr²×h₃) where h₃=h₁−h₂

Exchange Time

Exchange Time = Volume ÷ Flow Rate

If volume in gallons and flow in GPM → result in minutes.

Crest Flow (Weir Equation)

Q = Cw × L × H^(3/2)

Cw = weir coefficient, L = crest length (ft), H = head above crest (ft). Or use the hatchery lookup chart!

Camera Vision Method

H_pixels = y_crest − y_waterH_inches = H_pixels ÷ pixels_per_inchH_feet = H_inches ÷ 12Q_cfs = Cw × L × H_feet^1.5Q_gpm = Q_cfs × 448.831

The camera detects edges to find the crest line and water surface, measures the pixel distance, converts using calibration scale, then applies the weir equation.

Worked Examples

Example 1 — Raceway Volume

100 ft × 5 ft × 2.5 ft raceway:

V = 100 × 5 × 2.5 = 1,250 ft³ × 7.48 = 9,350 gallons

Example 2 — Exchange Time

9,350 gal raceway, 113 GPM flow (12" sump, 1" crest):

Time = 9,350 ÷ 113 = 82.7 min ≈ 1.4 hours

Example 3 — Round Tank

12 ft diameter, 4 ft deep:

r = 6, V = π × 36 × 4 = 452.4 ft³ = 3,384 gallons

Example 4 — Cone-Bottom Pond

20 ft diameter, h₁=6 ft, h₂=3 ft:

h₃ = 6−3 = 3 ft, r = 10 ftCyl: π×100×3 = 942.5 ft³Cone: ⅓×π×100×3 = 314.2 ft³Total = 1,256.6 ft³ = 9,400 gallons

Example 5 — Using the Crest Chart

10" sump, crest readings: 1.5", 1.25", 1.5"

Average = 1.42" → chart lookup ≈ 153–176 GPM → ~165 GPM

Why Flow Matters for Fish

O₂
Oxygen: Fresh water brings dissolved oxygen. Too slow = suffocation.
NH₃
Ammonia: Fish waste is toxic. Flow flushes it out.
°F
Temperature: Consistent flow = stable temperature = less stress.
[W]
Waste: Uneaten feed and feces must be flushed. Adequate velocity keeps rearing environments clean.

Salmonid hatcheries: 2-3 exchanges per hour (every 20-30 min)

[CAM] How the Camera Vision System Works

The Vision tab in this app uses computer vision — a branch of artificial intelligence where software "sees" through a camera and extracts measurements from the image. In hatcheries, the setup is perfect for this because the sump is fixed, the crest doesn't move, and the only thing changing is the water level.

Instead of manually reading a ruler against the sump every time, the camera does it automatically, continuously, and more consistently than a human eye.

[→]
The camera's only job: Measure H — the vertical distance between the crest (top of the sump pipe) and the water surface. Once you know H, the weir equation gives you flow: Q = Cw × L × H^(3/2)

The 7-Step Camera Pipeline

Here is exactly what happens, step by step, every time the camera runs:

Step 1 — Start Camera

The app requests access to your device camera (rear-facing preferred). The live video feed is drawn onto an HTML canvas element — this is like a digital drawing board that updates 30 times per second. The canvas is what lets us analyze each frame as raw pixel data.

[+]
Works with: Any smartphone, tablet, laptop webcam, Raspberry Pi camera, or GoPro. No special equipment needed.

Step 2 — Calibrate (Pixels → Real-World Inches)

The camera sees everything in pixels — tiny dots that make up the image. But we need measurements in inches. Calibration creates the conversion factor.

How it works:

Two orange lines appear on the video feed. You use the slider controls below the video to position them over a known distance on screen, then enter the real-world distance and click "Set Scale."

pixels_per_inch = pixel_distance_between_lines / known_distance_in_inches

Example: If the two lines are 300 pixels apart and you told it they span 6 inches:

pixels_per_inch = 300 / 6 = 50 px/inch

Now the app knows that every 50 pixels in the image equals 1 real-world inch. This calibration stays valid as long as the camera doesn't move.

Why calibration distance matters for accuracy:

Every time you position a slider, you might be off by a few pixels. The larger your calibration distance, the less those few pixels matter as a percentage of the total measurement:

1" calibration → ~6% error → +/- 15-20 GPM flow error 2" calibration → ~3% error → +/- 8-10 GPM flow error 4" calibration → ~1.5% error → +/- 4 GPM flow error 6" calibration → ~1% error → +/- 2 GPM flow error [RECOMMENDED]

The app defaults to 6 inches because it's the sweet spot: your crest heights range 0.5"–3", so a 6" reference spans double the measurement range, giving very precise pixels-per-inch across the entire range you care about. And 6" easily fits in the camera frame next to any sump size.

[+]
SETUP TIP: Tape a 6" section of a ruler (or a strip of masking tape with marks at 0" and 6") vertically on the sump pipe wall, right next to where the water overflows. This gives you a permanent calibration reference that's always in frame.

Step 3 — Auto-Detection (Finding Edges with the Sobel Operator)

This is the computer vision magic. Every video frame, the app needs to find two things: the crest line (top of the pipe) and the water surface.

How does a computer "see" an edge?

An edge is where the image suddenly changes from light to dark (or dark to light). The app uses a technique called the Sobel operator — a mathematical filter that detects these brightness changes.

The process:

  1. Convert to grayscale — the color image is converted to shades of gray (each pixel becomes a single brightness number from 0 = black to 255 = white)
  2. Apply the Sobel vertical gradient — for each pixel, the filter compares the brightness of pixels above and below it. A big difference = strong horizontal edge. The formula multiplies neighboring pixel brightness values by weights (-1, -2, -1 above; +1, +2, +1 below) to amplify edges
  3. Accumulate edge strength per row — the app adds up all the edge strengths across the center 60% of each horizontal row. This creates a "how strong is the edge at this height?" score for every row in the image
  4. Find the two strongest rows — scanning from top to bottom, the first strong edge is the crest (drawn in red). The next strong edge below it is the water surface (drawn in cyan)
[i]
Why this works in hatcheries: The crest is a sharp, straight edge between the pipe rim and the air/water. The water surface creates another contrast boundary where reflections meet the pipe wall. Both produce strong horizontal edges that the Sobel operator easily detects.

Step 4 — H Measurement (Pixels → Inches)

Now the app has two y-coordinates (vertical positions) in pixels:

y_crest = row position of the crest edge (e.g., 120 px from top) y_water = row position of the water surface (e.g., 195 px from top)

The crest height in pixels is the difference:

H_pixels = y_water − y_crest = 195 − 120 = 75 pixels

Convert to real-world inches using the calibration:

H_inches = H_pixels ÷ pixels_per_inch = 75 ÷ 50 = 1.5 inches

This is displayed on the video as a live orange arrow with the measurement label, updating every frame.

Step 5 — 30-Frame Averaging (Eliminating Wave Noise)

Water surfaces ripple and wave. A single frame might catch a ripple peak or trough, giving a wrong reading. The solution: average over 30 frames.

H_buffer = [1.48, 1.52, 1.50, 1.49, 1.53, 1.51, ...] (last 30 readings) H_averaged = sum(H_buffer) ÷ 30 = 1.505 inches

The buffer is a "sliding window" — as each new frame comes in, the oldest reading drops out and the newest is added. This smooths out wave noise while still tracking real changes in water level.

[#]
Why 30 frames? At 30 fps (frames per second), this averages over exactly 1 second of video — enough to smooth out typical water surface ripples without being so slow that it misses real level changes.

Step 6 — Dual Flow Calculation

With the averaged H value, the app calculates flow two independent ways simultaneously:

Method A — Weir Equation:

H_feet = H_inches ÷ 12 = 1.505 ÷ 12 = 0.1254 ft Q_cfs = Cw × L × H_feet^1.5 = 3.33 × 3.14 × 0.1254^1.5 = 0.464 cfs Q_gpm = 0.464 × 448.831 = 208 GPM

Method B — Hatchery Chart Interpolation:

The app looks up H_inches in the class reference chart (the same one you use manually). If the value falls between two chart rows, it linearly interpolates — for example, 1.505" falls between 1.5" (211 GPM) and 1.625" (240 GPM) for a 12" sump, so the interpolated value would be about 213 GPM.

[+]
Both values displayed on the HUD: This lets you cross-check the equation against the chart. If they're close, you know the reading is reliable.

Step 7 — Snap Reading (Capture & Record)

When you click "[SNAP] Capture," the app:

  1. Captures the current averaged H value from the 30-frame buffer
  2. Records both GPM calculations (equation and chart)
  3. Logs the number of frames that were averaged (for data quality)
  4. Timestamps the reading
  5. Adds it to the history table on the Vision page
  6. Resets the buffer so the next reading starts fresh

You can save any reading to the Data Log for export as CSV — useful for lab reports, shift logs, and long-term flow tracking.

Why This Matters — Traditional vs. Vision-Based

// Traditional Method
  1. Walk to sump
  2. Hold ruler across crest
  3. Read height by eye (parallax error)
  4. Write number down
  5. Walk to chart, look up GPM
  6. Record in logbook
!
Takes 2-5 min per reading. Subject to human error, parallax, and inconsistent technique.
// Vision Method
  1. Camera reads automatically
  2. Edge detection finds crest + water
  3. Pixel math converts to inches
  4. 30-frame average smooths waves
  5. Flow calculated instantly (both ways)
  6. One click saves to digital log
>
Instant, continuous, consistent. No parallax. Auto-averages wave noise. Digital record with timestamp.

The vision system doesn't replace understanding why the measurements matter — it just does the measuring part faster and more consistently. You still need to know the theory to interpret the results and make management decisions.
