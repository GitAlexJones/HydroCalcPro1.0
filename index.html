Sump Sizing
Calculate minimum sump volume from flow rate, retention time, and safety factor
Sump sizing uses the Continuity Equation: the sump must hold enough volume so water can reside long enough for treatment, settling, or processing. Volume = Flow Rate × Retention Time × Safety Factor In hatcheries, sumps collect drain water from raceways and tanks. Adequate retention time allows solids to settle and provides buffer volume for pump cycling. A safety factor of 1.25–2.0 is typical.

For RAS (Recirculating Aquaculture Systems), coldwater species like salmonids need tank turnover of ≤30 min, while warmwater species may use 60 min turnover.

The Continuity Equation & Conservation of Mass

All flow rate and sump calculations used in hatcheries, ponds, tanks, and plumbing trace back to a single foundational principle from fluid mechanics: the Continuity Equation, which is a direct application of the Conservation of Mass.

In its simplest form for incompressible fluids (like water), the Continuity Equation states that the volume of fluid flowing into a system must equal the volume flowing out, provided no fluid is added or removed.

Historical Foundation

Leonhard Euler (1707–1783)

Swiss mathematician who formalized the equations of fluid dynamics. His work established the mathematical framework for describing how fluids move under pressure and gravity, producing what are now known as the Euler equations of fluid motion.

Daniel Bernoulli (1700–1782)

Published Hydrodynamica in 1738, establishing the relationship between fluid speed, pressure, and height. Bernoulli's principle — that an increase in fluid speed occurs simultaneously with a decrease in pressure — combined with the Continuity Equation forms the basis of modern hydraulic engineering.

Modern Hydraulic Engineering

Their combined work became the foundation for pipe flow calculations, pump/sump design, weir flow measurement, hatchery engineering, and all modern fluid handling systems.

Core Equations

1. The Continuity Equation

For incompressible, steady-state flow through a closed conduit:

Q = A × v

Where Q = volumetric flow rate (m³/s or ft³/s), A = cross-sectional area (m² or ft²), and v = average velocity (m/s or ft/s).

For two points along the same flow path:

A₁ × v₁ = A₂ × v₂

This means if a pipe narrows, the velocity must increase. If it widens, velocity decreases. The total volume per second remains constant.

2. Weir Flow Equations

Weirs are used to measure and control flow in open channels. The discharge over a weir depends on the type of weir, the crest length, and the head of water above the crest.

Rectangular Weir: Q = Cw × L × H3/2

Where Cw is the weir coefficient (≈ 3.33 for sharp-crested, ≈ 3.09 for broad-crested), L is the crest length in feet, and H is the head above the crest in feet. The result Q is in cubic feet per second (cfs).

V-Notch Weir: Q = (8/15) × Cd × √(2g) × tan(θ/2) × H5/2

V-notch weirs are preferred for measuring low flow rates. A 90° notch with Cd ≈ 0.585 is the most common configuration.

Broad-Crested Weir: Q = C × L × H3/2  where  C = (2/3)3/2 × √g ≈ 1.705 (metric)

Broad-crested weirs create a critical depth condition over the crest. They are exceptionally stable for large discharge measurements and are commonly found in fish passage structures and stream gauging stations.

3. Sump Volume Equation

The minimum sump volume required to provide adequate retention time:

Vsump = Q × t × SF

Where Q = flow rate (GPM), t = desired retention time (minutes), and SF = safety factor (typically 1.25–2.0).

4. Water Exchange Rate

Used in aquaculture and recirculating systems to determine how quickly tank water is replaced:

Turnover Rate (turns/hr) = (Q × 60) / Vtank Turnover Time (min) = Vtank / Q

Where Q is flow rate in GPM and Vtank is tank volume in gallons.

Worked Examples

Example 1 — Pipe Flow (Continuity Equation)

Problem: Water flows through a 150 mm (0.15 m) diameter hatchery supply pipe at 2.5 m/s. Calculate the flow rate.

A = π × r² = π × (0.075)² = 0.01767 m² Q = A × v = 0.01767 × 2.5 = 0.04418 m³/s Q = 0.04418 × 15,850.3 = 700.2 GPM

Result: The pipe delivers approximately 700 gallons per minute — sufficient for a medium-scale hatchery raceway system.

Example 2 — Rectangular Weir Discharge

Problem: A hatchery pond outlet has a 3-foot-wide rectangular sharp-crested weir. The water level is 0.6 feet above the crest. What is the discharge?

Q = Cw × L × H3/2 = 3.33 × 3.0 × (0.6)1.5 Q = 3.33 × 3.0 × 0.4648 = 4.64 cfs Q = 4.64 × 448.83 = 2,082.6 GPM

Result: The weir discharges about 4.64 cubic feet per second (2,083 GPM).

Example 3 — Sump Sizing for Hatchery

Problem: A fish hatchery has 200 GPM flowing into its collection sump. The biologist wants 4 minutes of retention time with a 1.5× safety factor. The sump can be 3 feet deep. What dimensions are needed?

V = Q × t × SF = 200 × 4 × 1.5 = 1,200 gallons V = 1,200 / 7.48 = 160.4 ft³ Surface Area = 160.4 / 3 = 53.5 ft²

Result: A sump approximately 7.3 ft × 7.3 ft × 3 ft deep (or 8 ft × 7 ft × 3 ft for practical dimensions) would provide the required volume.

Example 4 — Exchange Rate for RAS

Problem: A salmonid recirculating system has a 2,000-gallon culture tank. The return pump runs at 80 GPM. Is the turnover rate adequate for coldwater species?

Turnover Time = V / Q = 2,000 / 80 = 25 min Turnover Rate = (80 × 60) / 2,000 = 2.4 turns/hr

Result: The 25-minute turnover time meets the guideline of ≤30 minutes for coldwater salmonid RAS. This is adequate flow for biofilter utilization and ammonia control.

Example 5 — V-Notch Weir for Low Flow

Problem: A 90° V-notch weir is used to measure low flow from a spring-fed hatchery supply. The head is 0.35 feet. What is the flow?

Q = (8/15) × 0.585 × √(2 × 32.174) × tan(45°) × (0.35)2.5 Q = 0.5333 × 0.585 × 8.024 × 1.0 × 0.07245 Q = 0.1814 cfs = 81.4 GPM

Result: The spring delivers about 81 GPM — enough to supply several small rearing troughs.
